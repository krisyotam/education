## Literary Theory and Criticism

### Declarative
- Major schools: Formalism/New Criticism, Structuralism, Post-Structuralism, Deconstruction, Marxist, Feminist, Psychoanalytic, Postcolonial, New Historicism, Reader-Response, Ecocriticism, Queer Theory, Disability Studies
- Key theorists and their central concepts (Saussure: signifier/signified; Barthes: death of the author; Derrida: differance; Foucault: discourse/power; Said: Orientalism; Butler: performativity; Spivak: subaltern)
- Periodization: when each school arose and what it reacted against

### Procedural
- Practice close reading with New Critical method (examine a poem without context)
- Write a Marxist reading of a novel, then a feminist reading of the same text
- Deconstruct a binary opposition in a short story
- Apply narratological concepts (Genette: focalization, analepsis, prolepsis) to a novel
- Write critical essays following academic conventions (MLA format, thesis-driven argument)

### Theory
- **Yale Open Course — Introduction to Theory of Literature** (Paul Fry): https://oyc.yale.edu/english/engl-300 — outstanding, the best free course on literary theory
- **Textbook**: *Literary Theory: An Introduction* by Terry Eagleton — accessible, opinionated, the standard intro
- **Textbook**: *Literary Theory: A Very Short Introduction* by Jonathan Culler — concise overview
- **Textbook**: *Literary Theory: An Anthology* ed. Rivkin & Ryan — comprehensive primary texts
- **Textbook**: *The Norton Anthology of Theory and Criticism* — the definitive collection of primary theoretical texts
- **Textbook**: *Beginning Theory* by Peter Barry — very accessible undergraduate introduction
- **Textbook**: *Critical Theory Today* by Lois Tyson — applies each theory to *The Great Gatsby*
- **Primary texts**: Aristotle's *Poetics*, Plato's *Republic* (Book X), Longinus *On the Sublime*, Sidney *Defence of Poesy*, Wordsworth's Preface to *Lyrical Ballads*, Coleridge *Biographia Literaria* (Ch. XIII-XIV), Shelley *Defence of Poetry*, T.S. Eliot "Tradition and the Individual Talent", Cleanth Brooks "The Heresy of Paraphrase", Barthes "Death of the Author", Foucault "What Is an Author?", Derrida "Structure, Sign, and Play", Said *Orientalism* (Introduction)

## Ancient Literature

### Declarative
- Epic conventions, oral-formulaic composition (Milman Parry, Albert Lord)
- Greek literary periods: Archaic, Classical, Hellenistic
- Key genres: epic, lyric, tragedy, comedy, history, philosophy, pastoral
- Major authors: Homer, Hesiod, Sappho, Pindar, Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes, Herodotus, Thucydides, Plato, Aristotle, Virgil, Ovid, Horace, Catullus, Lucretius, Seneca, Apuleius

### Procedural
- Compare Homeric and Virgilian epic (oral vs literary tradition)
- Analyze a Greek tragedy using Aristotle's *Poetics*
- Write on the concept of *kleos* (glory) across Greek epic and lyric
- Close read a passage of Ovid's *Metamorphoses* for intertextuality

### Theory
- **Primary reading (essential)**:
  - Homer: *Iliad*, *Odyssey* (Fagles or Lattimore translations)
  - Hesiod: *Theogony*, *Works and Days*
  - Sappho: fragments (Anne Carson's *If Not, Winter*)
  - Aeschylus: *Oresteia* (Agamemnon, Libation Bearers, Eumenides)
  - Sophocles: *Oedipus Rex*, *Antigone*, *Electra*
  - Euripides: *Medea*, *Bacchae*, *Hippolytus*
  - Aristophanes: *Clouds*, *Lysistrata*, *Frogs*
  - Plato: *Republic*, *Symposium*, *Phaedrus*
  - Aristotle: *Poetics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*
  - Virgil: *Aeneid* (Fagles or Fitzgerald)
  - Ovid: *Metamorphoses* (Mandelbaum or Humphries)
  - Horace: *Ars Poetica*, *Odes* (selections)
  - Lucretius: *De Rerum Natura*
  - Longus: *Daphnis and Chloe*; Apuleius: *The Golden Ass*
- **Secondary**: *The Cambridge Companion to Homer*, *The Cambridge Companion to Greek Tragedy*
- **Textbook**: *Classical Literature: A Very Short Introduction* by William Allan
- **Textbook**: *The Oxford History of the Classical World*

## Medieval Literature

### Declarative
- Periods: Early Medieval (5th-10th c.), High Medieval (11th-13th c.), Late Medieval (14th-15th c.)
- Key concepts: allegory, typology, courtly love (*fin'amors*), chivalric romance, estates satire, dream vision
- Major works and authors by language/tradition

### Procedural
- Analyze allegorical levels in Dante (literal, allegorical, moral, anagogical)
- Compare courtly love in troubadour lyric vs Chaucer
- Trace the Arthurian legend across multiple texts

### Theory
- **Primary reading**:
  - *Beowulf* (Seamus Heaney translation)
  - Dante: *Divine Comedy* (Inferno, Purgatorio, Paradiso) — Hollander or Mandelbaum translation
  - Chaucer: *Canterbury Tales* (especially General Prologue, Knight's Tale, Miller's Tale, Wife of Bath's Prologue & Tale, Pardoner's Tale)
  - *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight* (Simon Armitage translation)
  - Boccaccio: *Decameron* (selections)
  - Marie de France: *Lais*
  - Chretien de Troyes: *Yvain*, *Lancelot*
  - *The Song of Roland*
  - Malory: *Le Morte d'Arthur*
  - Julian of Norwich: *Revelations of Divine Love*
  - Langland: *Piers Plowman*
  - *The Thousand and One Nights* (selections)
- **Secondary**: *The Cambridge Companion to Medieval English Literature*, C.S. Lewis *The Discarded Image*, Auerbach *Mimesis* (Ch. 1-8)

## Romanticism

### Declarative
- Key concepts: imagination vs fancy, sublime, nature, individual genius, organic form, the Gothic
- First-generation Romantics: Blake, Wordsworth, Coleridge
- Second-generation: Byron, Shelley, Keats
- Continental: Goethe, Schiller, Novalis, Hugo
- American: Emerson, Thoreau, Whitman, Poe, Hawthorne, Melville

### Procedural
- Compare Wordsworth's and Coleridge's theories of imagination
- Analyze the Gothic in a novel (Shelley's *Frankenstein*, Stoker's *Dracula*)
- Close read a Keats ode for sensuous imagery and formal structure
- Write on the American Transcendentalist revision of European Romanticism

### Theory
- **Primary reading**:
  - Blake: *Songs of Innocence and Experience*, *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell*
  - Wordsworth: *Lyrical Ballads* (with Preface), *The Prelude* (1805 or 1850), "Tintern Abbey"

  - Byron: *Don Juan* (selections), *Childe Harold* (selections)
  - Shelley (P.B.): "Ozymandias", "Ode to the West Wind", "Mont Blanc", *Prometheus Unbound*
  - Keats: "Ode to a Nightingale", "Ode on a Grecian Urn", "To Autumn", letters
  - Mary Shelley: *Frankenstein*
  - Goethe: *Faust* (Part I), *The Sorrows of Young Werther*
  - Austen: *Pride and Prejudice*, *Emma* (bridges Enlightenment/Romantic)
  - Emerson: "Self-Reliance", "Nature"
  - Thoreau: *Walden*
  - Poe: tales ("The Fall of the House of Usher", "The Masque of the Red Death"), "The Raven", "The Philosophy of Composition"
  - Melville: *Moby-Dick*

## Modernism

### Declarative
- Key techniques: stream of consciousness, fragmentation, mythical method, imagism, unreliable narration
- Key movements: Imagism, Vorticism, Surrealism, Dada, Harlem Renaissance, Expressionism
- Major figures: Joyce, Woolf, Eliot, Pound, Kafka, Proust, Faulkner, Hemingway, Stein, Beckett, Borges
- Historical context: WWI, WWII, colonialism, modernity, alienation

### Procedural
- Analyze stream of consciousness in Woolf or Joyce
- Map the mythical method in *The Waste Land* or *Ulysses*
- Compare Hemingway's minimalism with Faulkner's maximalism

## Rhetoric and Composition

### Declarative
- Classical rhetoric: invention, arrangement, style, memory, delivery
- Aristotelian appeals: ethos, pathos, logos, kairos
- Tropes and figures: anaphora, chiasmus, antithesis, zeugma, litotes, hyperbole
- Modern composition theory: process approach, genre theory, rhetorical situation
